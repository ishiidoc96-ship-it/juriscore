Constitutional Law Supreme Court 2023 SCC 14

R. v. Morrison

A landmark decision clarifying the scope of reasonable expectation of privacy in digital communications accessed through third-party servers.

Facts

The respondent, Morrison, was investigated for alleged involvement in a narcotics trafficking ring. During the investigation, law enforcement intercepted textual communications stored on a secure, encrypted third-party server located outside the immediate jurisdiction.

The primary contention arose when the police accessed these records without a specific warrant targeting the server, relying instead on a general production order issued to the telecommunications provider. Morrison argued this constituted an unreasonable search and seizure under s. 8 of the Charter.

Issues

  • Did the accused possess a reasonable expectation of privacy in text messages stored on a third-party server after transmission?
  • Did the general production order provide sufficient lawful authority for the search and seizure of these specific records?
lightbulb

Ratio Decidendi

"An individual retains a reasonable expectation of privacy in digital communications stored on third-party servers, provided they have taken reasonable steps to ensure confidentiality (e.g., encryption). A general production order is insufficient to authorize the seizure of such deeply personal electronic records; specific judicial authorization targeting the server is required."

Holding

Appeal dismissed. The evidence obtained from the third-party server was excluded under s. 24(2) of the Charter, as its admission would bring the administration of justice into disrepute.
